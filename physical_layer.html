Amplitude Modulation (AM)
ANALOG
▼
AM encodes information by changing the signal's amplitude (its strength or height). A high amplitude might represent a logical 1, while a low amplitude represents 0. The carrier frequency remains constant — only the power changes.

Characteristics

Sinusoidal carrier Varying voltage Noise-sensitive
Frequency Modulation (FM)
ANALOG
▼
FM encodes logical values by varying the frequency of the carrier wave. A high frequency might represent a 1, a low frequency a 0. The amplitude stays constant, making FM more resistant to noise than AM.

Characteristics

Constant amplitude Frequency shifts Noise-resistant
Phase Modulation (PM)
ANALOG
▼
PM encodes information by modifying the phase of the carrier wave. A phase shift of 180° can represent a change in bit value. Both amplitude and frequency remain constant — only the wave's starting position changes.

Characteristics

Phase shifts Used in QAM Wi-Fi / 4G
NRZ — Non-Return-to-Zero
DIGITAL
▼
NRZ uses distinct electrical voltage levels to represent 0s and 1s. High voltage = 1, Low voltage = 0. The signal never "returns to zero" between bits. Simple and efficient but can have synchronization issues with long runs of the same bit.

Characteristics

Square pulses Two voltage levels DC component
Manchester Encoding
DIGITAL
▼
Manchester encoding represents logic based on transitions: low-to-high = 1, high-to-low = 0. Every bit period contains a transition, enabling the receiver to synchronize its clock automatically. Used in Ethernet (10BASE-T).

Characteristics

Self-clocking No DC bias 10BASE-T Ethernet
⟹⟹⟹

Parallel Transmission

Multiple bits are sent simultaneously across separate channels. Faster but requires more physical wires and suffers from signal skew over distance.

⟹

Serial Transmission

Bits are sent one at a time over a single channel. Simpler cabling, longer distances. Converters switch between parallel and serial as needed by hardware.

02 — Modes & Synchronization

How data flows
through a channel

→

Simplex

One direction only. Like a broadcast radio — the receiver can never reply.

⇄

Half-Duplex

Both directions, but not simultaneously. One side transmits while the other listens — like a walkie-talkie.

⇆

Full-Duplex

Simultaneous two-way communication. Both parties can talk and listen at the same time — like a phone call.

🔀

Asynchronous Sync

Characters are sent irregularly, each wrapped in Start and Stop bits so the receiver knows where a character begins and ends. Simple and cheap but relatively slow — used in serial ports (RS-232).

🕐

Synchronous Sync

Bits are emitted at regular intervals using a shared clock signal. No start/stop overhead — continuous streams at high data rates. Required for high-speed links and fiber optics.

Multiplexing — sharing one medium between many users
TDM — Time Division Multiplexing

Each user gets a cyclic time slot. Everyone takes turns — like a round-robin token. Simple to implement, wastes bandwidth if a user has nothing to send.

FDM — Frequency Division Multiplexing

Bandwidth is divided into separate frequency channels, one per user. All users transmit simultaneously on their own frequency — like FM radio stations.

03 — Data Rates & Metrics

How much data can
a channel carry?

📶

Bandwidth (W)

The range of frequencies a channel can transmit, calculated as the difference between upper and lower frequency limits. Measured in Hz. Higher bandwidth = more data capacity.

🔢

Valence (V)

The number of distinct signal states. With V states, each symbol carries Q = log₂V bits of information. Binary uses V=2, giving 1 bit per symbol.

📏

Symbol Rate (Baud)

State changes per second. The Nyquist Criterion defines the theoretical maximum as 2 × W symbols/second for a noiseless channel.

📡

S/N Ratio

Signal-to-Noise ratio. The higher the S/N, the more reliably data can be transmitted. Fundamental to Shannon's Theorem.

04 — Transmission Media

The physical path
data travels through

🔌
Wired

Twisted Pair

Two insulated copper wires twisted together. UTP (unshielded) and STP (shielded) variants. Uses RJ45 connectors. The most common LAN medium. Susceptible to interference but cheap and easy to install.

📺
Wired

Coaxial Cable

Central conductor surrounded by insulation and a braided shield. Uses BNC connectors. Low interference, higher bandwidth than twisted pair. Used in cable TV and older Ethernet (10BASE2).

💡
Wired

Optical Fiber

Transmits data as light pulses. Extremely high bandwidth, immune to electromagnetic interference, supports very long distances. Ideal for backbone networks and intercontinental links.

📶
Wireless

Radio Waves (Wi-Fi)

Uses electromagnetic radio waves. 802.11ax (Wi-Fi 6) is a current standard, offering multi-gigabit speeds. Range limited by walls, interference, and distance.

🔦
Wireless

Laser (Line-of-Sight)

High-bandwidth laser beam transmission. Requires a clear optical path between sender and receiver. Used in point-to-point building-to-building links and free-space optical communication.

Each medium offers different tradeoffs in speed, distance, cost, and interference resistance.

04b — Interconnection Devices

Hardware at
the Physical Layer

NIC

Network Interface Card

Connects a device to the network. Identified by a unique 6-byte MAC address burned in at manufacture. Every networked device has one — the fundamental identity at Layer 2.

Hub

Signal Repeater / Broadcaster

Receives a signal from one port and broadcasts it to all other ports. Acts as a repeater/amplifier. No intelligence — no routing, no filtering. Creates a single collision domain.

Modem

Modulator-Demodulator

Converts digital computer data to analog signals for transmission over analog networks (like telephone lines), then converts back. Essential for DSL and cable internet connections.
